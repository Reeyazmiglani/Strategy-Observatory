## Why Everything Is Becoming a Platform (Even When It Shouldn’t)

### 1. Context

“Platform” has quietly become one of the most overused words in business. What used to describe a very specific type of company is now used almost everywhere. A payments app wants to be a platform. A SaaS tool talks about building an ecosystem. Even companies that started with one clear function now aim to become the place where everything happens. This shift has happened quickly. Around 2015–2020, the platform model was still associated with a few companies like Amazon, Apple, and Google. By 2023–2026, it has become the default ambition across industries. The assumption now is simple: if the most valuable companies are platforms, then becoming one must be the way to scale. But that logic skips an important step.


### 2. What Is Actually Happening

The platform model works because it changes how growth happens.

Take Amazon. As of 2024, more than 60% of units sold on Amazon come from third-party sellers, not Amazon itself. That means Amazon is no longer just selling products. It is facilitating transactions between others and taking a cut. Apple operates in a similar way. Its App Store generated over $85 billion in developer billings and sales in 2023, with Apple taking a commission on many of those transactions. The company does not create most of the apps, but it controls the system in which they exist. Microsoft has taken this even further in enterprise software. Products like Azure and Microsoft 365 are not just tools. They are layers on which other companies build their own operations.

This is the core shift. These companies are not just producing value. They are positioning themselves where value flows. As Bill Gurley, a well-known investor, once put it: “The best businesses are not just products, they are platforms that enable others.” That idea has shaped strategy across industries.


### 3. Why Everyone Wants to Do It

From a capital perspective, the attraction is obvious. Platforms scale differently from traditional businesses. Instead of increasing output internally, they grow by increasing participation externally. More users bring more producers. More producers increase variety. More variety attracts more users. This creates network effects. You can see this clearly in companies like Uber and Airbnb. Uber does not own cars, and Airbnb does not own hotels. Yet both have scaled globally because they sit between supply and demand. Shopify offers another example. By enabling merchants to build online stores and integrating payments, logistics, and apps, it has created a system where thousands of businesses operate on top of its infrastructure. For investors, this model is powerful. Platforms often generate higher margins, stronger retention, and longer-term dominance. That is why capital continues to flow into companies that successfully position themselves this way. The conclusion many companies draw is straightforward: if platforms create a durable advantage, then becoming one is the next logical step.

### 4. Where It Starts Breaking

The problem is that platforms are not just built by intention. They require specific conditions. At a minimum, you need two or more sides that depend on each other, enough activity to sustain interaction, and a reason for participants to stay. Many companies try to replicate the structure without meeting these conditions.

For example, several fintech firms in India and Southeast Asia attempted to build “super apps” between 2020 and 2024 by combining payments, lending, commerce, and services. While adoption was initially strong, usage often remained fragmented because the different services did not naturally reinforce each other. Similarly, many SaaS companies introduced marketplaces for integrations or third-party tools. In theory, this creates an ecosystem. In practice, many of these marketplaces see limited activity because the core product does not generate enough interaction to sustain it. The structure exists, but the system does not.

What looks like expansion is often just layering.


### 5. The Capital Allocation Angle

Turning a business into a platform is not just a strategic decision. It is a capital allocation decision. Building a platform requires investing in infrastructure, attracting both sides of the market, and often subsidizing early participation. Uber, for example, spent years offering incentives to both drivers and riders before reaching stable unit economics in many markets. These investments are made with the expectation of long-term payoff. Once the system reaches scale, the economics improve significantly. Margins expand, and the platform becomes harder to compete with. But when the system fails to reach that scale, the capital does not convert into an advantage. It remains tied up in features, integrations, and expansions that do not generate meaningful returns. Between 2021 and 2024, many technology companies expanded aggressively into platform-like models, only to later scale back due to weak usage or rising costs. The capital was deployed, but the structure did not hold.

This is where the difference between platform ambition and platform reality becomes clear.


### 6. Bigger Thought

What has changed is not just the success of platforms, but how broadly the model is now applied. Instead of asking whether a business should become a platform, many companies begin with that assumption and work backward. That shift affects how companies design products, allocate capital, and compete. But not every business benefits from becoming a system. In many cases, value comes from focus, not expansion. From doing one thing well, rather than trying to sit between everything. Platforms are powerful because they concentrate activity. But when applied without structure, they can just as easily dilute it.


### Closing Thought

The platform model is one of the most powerful structures in modern markets. But it works because of how it is built, not because of how often it is copied. And the difference between the two usually shows up in how capital is deployed, long before it shows up in results.
