## Why Is the Yen Everywhere All of a Sudden?

### 1. Context

I first started paying attention to the yen carry trade during my winter internship at Invasset, around the time markets were watching the Bank of Japan’s December 19 policy meeting closely. What stayed with me was not just the topic itself, but the mood around it. Inside the office, people were paying quiet attention to something that felt almost invisible outside finance. Nobody sounded alarmed. Nobody sounded relaxed either. It felt like watching people notice a shift before anyone else sees it.

That was the first time I felt how markets sometimes move the way crowds move. Not suddenly. Not emotionally. But because everyone begins adjusting at once.

### 2. The Trade in Simple Terms

Once I understood the yen carry trade, the logic felt surprisingly human. For years, borrowing yen was extremely cheap, so investors used it to invest elsewhere where returns were higher. But what makes it powerful is not the idea itself. It is the scale and timing.

If I had to explain it simply, it reminds me of how crowds behave in a crowded station. Nobody plans to move together. But when space tightens even slightly, everyone adjusts at the same time.
That is how I started seeing the trade. Not as a financial idea, but as collective behavior.

### 3. Why December Actually Mattered

By December 2024, the Bank of Japan had already exited negative rates earlier in March, but markets were not focused on what had happened. They were focused on what could happen next. Carry trades depend on funding stability, not just funding levels. That distinction is important. Even small uncertainty around future policy creates pressure because leveraged positions cannot tolerate ambiguity for long. December felt different because positioning was stretched. Investors were not reacting to the rate itself. They were reacting to the possibility that the entire funding environment was shifting. That was the moment I understood the trade was less about Japan and more about global balance sheets.

### 4. How It Actually Moves Markets

The carry trade matters because it sits underneath multiple asset classes simultaneously. When funding conditions remain stable, leverage expands quietly across equities, credit, and emerging markets. Risk looks contained because liquidity is abundant.

But when funding conditions tighten even slightly, the adjustment happens fast. Positions unwind, volatility rises, and correlations increase across assets. We saw this clearly in August 2024. Yen volatility spiked, global equities sold off briefly, and funding-sensitive assets moved together. The speed of the move looked emotional, but it was mechanical. Markets were not reacting to headlines. They were reacting to positioning constraints.

### 5. Bigger Thought

What struck me most was how different this was from the way markets are usually explained. Most commentary focuses on narratives. But large moves often have less to do with stories and more to do with structure. The carry trade is not interesting because it is dramatic. It is interesting because it forces behavior.

Once I understood that, market movements started making more sense.

### Closing Thought

That December was the first time I understood how much of global markets is driven by mechanics rather than emotion.
Since then, I pay less attention to explanations built around events and more attention to positioning, leverage, and funding conditions underneath them.
